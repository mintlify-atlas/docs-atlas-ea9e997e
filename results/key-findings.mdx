## Overview

The exploratory data analysis (EDA) of TelecomX's customer base identified several statistically significant drivers of churn. The findings below are derived from a cleaned dataset of **7,043 records** (after removing ~3% with missing churn labels) and quantified using Pearson correlation, grouped churn rates, and distribution analysis.

## Finding 1 — Contract Type

Contract type is one of the most powerful predictors of churn. Customers on month-to-month contracts represent **55%** of the customer base but have a dramatically higher likelihood of leaving.

| Contract Type   | Share of Customers | Churn Rate |
|-----------------|--------------------|------------|
| Month-to-month  | 55.0%              | 42.7%      |
| One year        | 20.9%              | 11.3%      |
| Two year        | 24.1%              | 2.8%       |

<Note>
  Customers on annual or biennial contracts are 4–15× less likely to churn than those on month-to-month plans. The absence of a long-term commitment is the single strongest categorical predictor of churn.
</Note>

## Finding 2 — Tenure (Customer Longevity)

Tenure (months with the company) has the **strongest negative correlation with churn** at **-0.35**. Churn risk is heavily concentrated in the early months of the customer relationship.

- Customers in their **first 1–6 months** show the highest churn density in KDE distribution analysis.
- Customers who survive past the 12-month mark show a dramatically lower and gradually decreasing churn probability.
- Long-tenured customers (high `Charges.Total`) have a **-0.20** correlation with churn, confirming that cumulative investment in the service signals loyalty.

<Tip>
  The tenure distribution confirms a classic "bathtub curve" pattern: high early churn, stable mid-tenure, very low late-tenure churn. Early retention investment has the highest marginal return.
</Tip>

## Finding 3 — Tech Support Subscription

Customers without a TechSupport subscription churn at much higher rates than those who subscribe. The analysis of categorical variables shows TechSupport as one of the most discriminating service features.

| TechSupport Status | Churn Behavior         |
|--------------------|------------------------|
| No subscription    | High churn rate        |
| Subscribed         | Significantly lower churn |
| No internet service| Near-zero churn        |

This "retention anchor" effect also extends to other value-added services: customers with more subscriptions (OnlineSecurity, OnlineBackup, DeviceProtection) churn less, consistent with an **"ecosystem effect"** — the more services a customer uses, the higher the perceived switching cost.

## Finding 4 — Internet Service Type

Internet service type shows a notable churn differential. Despite being a premium service, Fiber optic customers churn more than DSL customers.

| Internet Service | Share of Customers | Churn Pattern          |
|------------------|--------------------|------------------------|
| Fiber optic      | 44.0%              | Higher churn rate      |
| DSL              | 34.4%              | Lower churn rate       |
| No internet      | 21.7%              | Very low churn rate    |

<Warning>
  Fiber optic's higher churn despite premium pricing suggests price-value dissatisfaction. Customers paying more for Fiber optic may be more likely to compare competitors and switch if the service quality does not justify the cost.
</Warning>

## Finding 5 — Monthly Charges

Monthly charges correlate **positively with churn** at **+0.19**. Customers with higher monthly bills are more price-sensitive and more likely to churn.

- Churned customers have a **higher median monthly charge** than retained customers (visible in boxplot distributions).
- The daily charge metric (`Charges.Daily = Charges.Monthly / 30`) confirms the same pattern at finer granularity.
- In contrast, total charges (`Charges.Total`) correlate **negatively** with churn at **-0.20**, because high total spend reflects long tenure, not high monthly cost.

| Metric              | Correlation with Churn | Interpretation                              |
|---------------------|------------------------|---------------------------------------------|
| `Charges.Monthly`   | +0.19                  | Higher monthly bill → higher churn risk     |
| `Charges.Total`     | -0.20                  | Higher total spend → longer tenure → lower risk |
| `tenure`            | -0.35                  | Longer tenure → much lower churn risk       |

## Finding 6 — Demographics

### Senior Citizens

`SeniorCitizen` customers (age ≥ 65, encoded as `1`) show a higher churn rate than non-senior customers. The categorical analysis grouped by this variable shows a clear imbalance in churn distribution.

### Partner and Dependents

Customers without a `Partner` or `Dependents` are more likely to churn. Customers with household ties tend to be more stable — likely reflecting broader financial stability, longer-term planning, or the practical burden of switching providers for an entire household.

| Demographic Factor | Churn Direction                        |
|--------------------|----------------------------------------|
| SeniorCitizen = 1  | Higher churn rate                      |
| Partner = No       | Higher churn rate                      |
| Dependents = No    | Higher churn rate                      |
| Gender             | No significant difference in churn rate |

<Note>
  Gender (`gender`) shows no meaningful difference in churn rates across male and female customers — it is not a useful predictor variable for retention modeling.
</Note>
